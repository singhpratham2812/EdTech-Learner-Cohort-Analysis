## Overview

Account Takeover (ATO) fraud happens when a fraudster gains access to a legitimate user's account using stolen credentials. Unlike signup fraud, it is invisible at the account level — the login uses a valid password and looks normal to basic monitoring systems.

This project uses Python to analyse 5,000 login events, engineer 5 behavioural fraud signals per event, combine them into a risk score, and produce an actionable watchlist of high-risk logins for investigator review.

> **Real-world context:** This replicates the ATO detection logic I applied manually as a Risk Analyst at Noon — reviewing login anomalies, device mismatches, IP inconsistencies, and off-hours behaviour. This project automates those same signals in Python, making them scalable across thousands of events.

---

## Problem Statement

Given 5,000 login events across 300 user accounts, can we:
1. Detect which logins show behavioural signals inconsistent with the account's normal pattern?
2. Score each login using multiple signals combined into a single risk score?
3. Classify logins into High / Medium / Low risk tiers?
4. Export a prioritised watchlist for the fraud operations team?

## The 5 Fraud Signals

Each signal compares the current login event against the user's known normal behaviour pattern. Output is 0 (normal) or 1 (suspicious).

| # | Signal | Logic | Points | Why it matters |
|---|--------|-------|--------|----------------|
| 1 | New Device | `device_type != primary_device` | +1 | Real users log in from their usual device. A new device means someone else's hardware |
| 2 | New Country | `country != primary_country` | +1 | A login from Russia on an India-only account is a geo anomaly — impossible travel |
| 3 | Off Hours Login | `hour < usual_start OR hour > usual_end` | +1 | Fraudsters operate at 3am. Real users log in during their normal hours |
| 4 | High Failed Attempts | `failed_attempts > 2` | +1 | More than 2 wrong passwords = credential guessing or brute force |
| 5 | High Value Transaction | `transaction_amount > 10,000` | +1 | Fraudsters move money immediately. Normal users browse before transacting |

**Risk score = sum of all 5 signals (0–5)**

| Risk Tier | Score | Action |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| High | 4–5 | Review immediately |
| Medium | 2–3 | Review today |
| Low | 0–1 | Monitor only |

## Key Findings

**Finding 1 — Every ATO event triggered all 5 signals simultaneously**

All 741 ATO events scored 4 or 5 — triggering new device, new country, off hours, high failed attempts, and high transaction together. The signals are highly correlated, making the combined risk score extremely reliable.

**Finding 2 — Zero false positives in the High Risk tier**

Model precision = 100%. Every login flagged as High Risk was confirmed ATO. This is a product of the synthetic dataset design — in a real-world deployment, some false positives are expected and threshold tuning would be needed.

**Finding 3 — ATO logins cluster between midnight and 6am**

The login hour histogram shows a clear spike in ATO attempts during off-hours. Normal logins concentrate between 9am–8pm. Time-of-day monitoring alone catches a significant portion of attacks.

**Finding 4 — 100% of ATO events included a high-value transaction**

Every ATO login was followed by a transaction above 10,000. Fraudsters move money immediately after access — they do not browse. This makes the transaction signal the most financially critical flag for loss prevention.

## Limitations

| Limitation | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Synthetic data | Signals are perfectly separated — real data has noise and overlap |
| Rule-based model | Fixed thresholds don't adapt to user behaviour changes over time |
| No velocity check | Multiple rapid logins from same account not yet detected |
| No IP reputation | Known bad IPs not flagged — would need external threat intelligence feed |
